Barking Binaries Don't Bite

The lifecycles of stars are determined by their masses and chemical compositions. More massive stars burn through their fuel quicker than lower mass stars, and being hotter, have a blue color. Collections of stars that are gravitationally bound together are called star clusters. The stars in these clusters formed at the same time and with the same chemical composition but with different masses.


NGC 188 is a rich, old, open cluster that has one of the most well-characterized populations that trace out stellar evolution. Interestingly, this cluster (among others) also reveals stars that deviate from traditional single-star evolution, challenging our models of how stars change over their lifetimes. Blue Straggler stars (BSSs) are the most common stellar evolution pathway, appearing bluer and brigher than other stars in the cluster. Their temperatures and brightnesses correspond to those of more massive stars, indicating that they should have burned through fuel much quicker than their peers in the cluster.

My research tries to understand how these stars form.

BSSs are thought to have been formed through interactions between binary stars–a pair of stars that orbit each other–with mass transfer between the binary companions being the most prevelant formation mechanism. Binary stars often can have unequal masses (which means that one star can evolve faster than its companion).

As the more massive star in a binary evolves from the main sequence (core hydrogen fusion occurs during this time) to become a red giant (shell hydrogen fusion), its outer layers begin to expand and fill its Roche lobe, the region around a star within which the orbiting material is gravitationally bound to that star. Once the star fills this region, any further expansion will cause the mass of the giant to flow onto its companion. As more mass accumulates on the accreting star, it becomes bluer and brighter. Ultimately the donor is left with nothing but its hot, exposed core (white-dwarf).

Many post-transfer systems hide their white-dwarf companions; their main-sequence counterpart is simply brighter in the light humans can see. However, a mild far-UV excess, the wavelengths of light where the white-dwarf dominates, is a classic signature even when the WD is invisible in optical light. These mass-transfer remnants often sit at long periods (hundreds to thousands of days). Sometimes these stars go around each other in a circular orbit, other times in an eccentric (elliptical) orbit. Understanding the mechanism(s) of mass transfer and its role in producing different outcomes is still an open question today.
